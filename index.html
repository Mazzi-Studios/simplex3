The Science

Four receptors. One molecule.

The root cause of GLP-class GI side effects is not appetite suppression — it is delayed gastric emptying. GLP-1 receptor activation slows gastric motility as a mechanism of satiety. At therapeutic doses, this slowing produces nausea, vomiting, and in severe cases, gastroparesis.

Ghrelin receptor partial agonism is the only known mechanism that directly counteracts this effect — validated in peer-reviewed research as a prokinetic agent that accelerates gastric emptying through GHS-R1a activation.

Real-world user data at 7.5mg of SIMPLEX-G2 (same ghrelin prokinetic mechanism) demonstrates clear appetite suppression with no GLP-class GI symptoms — validating the ghrelin prokinetic hypothesis in a human model before Simplex3 even reached formal trial.

Simplex3 incorporates a proprietary ghrelin fragment (Gly-Ser-Dap(N-octanoyl)-Phe-NH₂) appended to a retatrutide-derived GIP/GLP-1/glucagon backbone via PEG4 spacer, with C18 diacid lipidation at Lys17 for once-weekly half-life targeting.

Frequently Asked Questions

What researchers ask first.

What makes Simplex3 different from retatrutide?
Simplex3 adds a fourth receptor target — the ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a) — to the GIP/GLP-1/Glucagon backbone retatrutide pioneered. The ghrelin partial agonism is prokinetic, which directly counteracts the delayed gastric emptying that drives GI side effects in every GLP-class compound. Same metabolic backbone, with a tolerability mechanism no other compound offers.
Why include the ghrelin receptor in your peptide?
The root cause of nausea, vomiting, and gastroparesis in GLP-class therapy is delayed gastric emptying caused by GLP-1 receptor activation. Ghrelin receptor partial agonism is the only known mechanism that directly accelerates gastric motility through GHS-R1a — validated in peer-reviewed research as a prokinetic agent. It is the tolerability solution no other compound offers.

What is the half-life of Simplex3?
Simplex3 incorporates C18 diacid lipidation at Lys17 to enable albumin binding, targeting a once-weekly dosing half-life consistent with the retatrutide and tirzepatide pharmacokinetic class. Specific half-life characterization is part of ongoing research; no human trial data exists for Simplex3.
